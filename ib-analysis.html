IB History Analysis

Cold War connections

The Berlin Wall connects to the IB Cold War theme because it shows how alliances, ideology, propaganda, and military pressure shaped everyday life.

Main thesis

The Berlin Wall turned the Cold War from an abstract conflict into a physical division. It separated families and neighborhoods, but it also separated two political systems: Western capitalism and Soviet-backed communism.

MANIA connections

MANIA is usually used for World War I, but it also works as a way to organize the pressures around Berlin.

Militarism

The Wall was guarded with soldiers, watchtowers, patrols, and barriers. Military force was used not just against foreign enemies, but against people trying to leave East Germany.

Alliances

This is the strongest connection. West Berlin was protected by the United States, Britain, and France and tied to the Western alliance system. East Berlin was part of the Soviet bloc and connected to the Warsaw Pact.

Nationalism

Both sides claimed to represent Germany's true future. West Germany showed democratic capitalism, while East Germany claimed to represent anti-fascist socialism.

Imperialism

Berlin showed how superpowers influenced smaller states after World War II. The Soviet Union dominated much of Eastern Europe, while the United States supported Western Europe politically, economically, and militarily.

Assassination

This part fits less directly. There was no single assassination that caused the Wall. The better point is that the Wall came from long-term tension, fear, and political crisis instead of one sudden event.

Stalin

Stalin helped create the Cold War conditions that made Berlin a flashpoint. During the Berlin Blockade, the Soviet Union tried to pressure the Western Allies out of West Berlin. The blockade failed, but it proved how important Berlin was to both sides.

Gorbachev

Gorbachev's reforms changed the Soviet bloc. Glasnost and perestroika weakened strict Soviet control and made it harder for communist governments to stop reform movements with force.

1948 Arab-Israeli War

The connection is not that Berlin caused the war. The stronger connection is that both came from the unstable post-World War II order, where borders, refugees, new states, and superpower interests shaped global politics.
