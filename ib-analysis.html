IB History Analysis

Why the Wall fits the Cold War

The Berlin Wall connects directly to the IB Cold War theme because it shows how alliances, ideology, propaganda, and military pressure shaped ordinary life. It also connects to authoritarian states because East Germany used surveillance and force to control movement.

Main thesis

The Berlin Wall mattered because it turned the Cold War from an abstract ideological conflict into a physical object: a guarded border that divided families, governments, and two competing visions of the world.

Stalin connection

Stalin helped create the early Cold War conditions that made Berlin a flashpoint. During the Berlin Blockade of 1948-1949, the Soviet Union tried to pressure the Western Allies out of West Berlin. The failure of the blockade did not create the Wall immediately, but it showed how Berlin could become a test of power between East and West.

Gorbachev connection

Gorbachev did not personally knock down the Wall, but his reforms changed the atmosphere of Eastern Europe. His ideas of glasnost and perestroika weakened strict Soviet control and made it harder for communist governments to use force the way they had before.

1948 Arab-Israeli War connection

The connection is not that the Berlin Wall caused the war. The stronger link is that both came from the unstable post-World War II world. Borders were redrawn, new states formed, refugees were created, and the U.S. and USSR increasingly viewed regional conflicts through Cold War strategy.

Berlin shows how the Cold War was not only about weapons or speeches; it was also about who controlled space, movement, identity, and the right to leave.
