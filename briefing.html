Overview

The Berlin Wall

The setup

After World War II, Germany was divided into four occupation zones. Berlin, sitting deep inside the Soviet zone, was also split among the four powers. The result was a Western-aligned city completely surrounded by a communist state. For East Germans, it was also the most visible escape route to the West.

Why it was built

Between 1949 and 1961, roughly 2.7 million people left East Germany, most through Berlin. The government built the Wall overnight on August 13, 1961 to close that route. The official line was protection from Western fascists. The real reason was stopping a population drain that was destabilizing the country.

What the Wall actually was

Not just one wall. The full system included an inner wall, a floodlit death strip with mines and tripwires, an outer wall, watchtowers every 300 meters, guard dogs, and armed patrols under shoot-on-sight orders. 155 km total length. Over 100 people were killed attempting to cross it.

What it represented

A government building a prison around its own citizens is a hard image to spin. East Germany tried anyway, calling the Wall an "anti-fascist protective rampart." Nobody believed it. For the next 28 years, the Wall was the most effective piece of anti-communist propaganda the West never had to create.
